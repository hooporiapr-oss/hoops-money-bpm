03 ON-COURT APPLICATIONS
What these scores translate to in live basketball situations
1
Reading the pick-and-roll as the on-ball defender
With a strong Recall score (L7) and mid-tier Reflex (L5), Marcus is better at remembering the coverage call than at processing multiple cues at once. He'll execute a drop coverage cleanly when the play develops linearly. He'll get hurt by a screen-reject-reset or a ghost screen — situations where multiple cues arrive at once and he has to brake on a new read before the first one finishes.
2
Closeout on the catch-and-shoot
Marcus's React score (L6) is solid. The single-beat brake read — defender recognizes the kickout, commits to closeout — arrives on time at game tempo. The closeout itself should be clean. The risk is the follow-up: if the shooter pump-fakes and drives baseline, Marcus's lower Ritmo (L5) means his mid-brake redirection will be late. Expect clean closeouts but vulnerability to the shot-fake-drive.
3
Running the offense — holding the set across rotations
This is where Marcus's profile shines. A Level 7 Recall means he holds the possession sequence across multiple rotations without dropping the thread. He'll run sets cleanly, remember the secondary and tertiary actions, and reset the offense when the primary option dies. Point guards with high Recall are the ones who make other players look organized.
4
Hesitation moves and rhythm-timed pullups
This is Marcus's weakest area. A Level 3 Beat score means his brake timing doesn't land on rhythm — his hesitations will look mechanical, his pullups will arrive a half-beat late. Defenders with good feel will read his pattern. This is a development priority for offensive scoring.
5
Sequential play execution in late-clock situations
Late-clock sets demand sequential brake execution — set the screen, make the read, execute the counter, then the re-screen, then the pop. Marcus's Level 4 Replay means his execution on sequential chains breaks down as the chain gets longer. He'll run the first two actions well and lose the third. In late-clock situations, expect early execution to be clean and late execution to hurry or skip a step.
04 DEVELOPMENT PRIORITIES
Ranked training focus to move the composite Brake level up
01
The Beat — Rhythm-timed braking (L3 → L5 target)
Marcus's biggest cognitive leverage point. Rhythm-timed braking is the layer that makes hesitation moves, pullups, and mid-play pace changes readable or unreadable to defenders. Four weeks of daily Beat drill work at 60 BPM, then build to 90 BPM.
02
The Replay — Sequential execution (L4 → L6 target)
Sequential brake execution is the foundation of running sets, late-clock execution, and complex coverage schemes. Marcus's Recall is strong (he remembers the sequence) but his Replay is weak (he struggles to execute it in order). This is a closable gap — sequence execution develops faster than rhythm timing.
03
The Reflex — Multi-element reads at tempo (L5 → L6 target)
Marcus's Reflex score is fine at 60 BPM but drops off at 90 BPM — meaning his multi-cue processing doesn't hold up at game tempo. This is the layer that handles ghost screens, hesi-into-pick plays, and simultaneous weakside action. Progressive tempo work — add 90 BPM Reflex once The Beat foundation is laid.
05 WHAT THIS MEASURES
The scope of this report — what BPM measures and what it does not

The Brake measures the cognitive layer of basketball braking. It quantifies how the player reads the play, processes cues, remembers sequences, detects changes, and times brake commitments at three tempos. These are specific, measurable cognitive inputs to on-court deceleration.

The Brake does not measure physical braking capacity — eccentric strength, landing mechanics, deceleration force absorption, or injury tolerance. These are trained separately through isoinertial protocols (see below). The Brake also does not measure applied basketball skill — shooting, ball-handling, defensive technique, basketball IQ as traditionally assessed, or athletic ability.

The Brake is a measurement, not a scouting grade. A high composite Brake level does not predict that a player will make varsity, earn a scholarship, or succeed at the next level. A low composite Brake level does not preclude any of those outcomes. The Brake identifies cognitive braking strengths and gaps — what to train, not who to recruit.

06 PHYSICAL BRAKE LAYER
Complementary physical brake training — outside BPM's measurement scope
Physical layer pointer
BPM measures the cognitive brake. The physical brake — the body's capacity to actually absorb and decelerate force — is trained separately through isoinertial flywheel protocols. Cognitive reads without physical absorption produces late brakes and injury risk. The physical layer is how the brake lands clean.
